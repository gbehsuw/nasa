Energy in British Columbia

What is SMAP?

SMAP is an orbiting observatory launched by NASA that measures the amount of water in soil across the Earth. Using this data, we can track floods and droughts in certain parts of the world. Since British Columbia is susceptible to forest fires due to droughts, we can use SMAP to confirm this.

Why are we using it?

By using the information provided by SMAP, we can analyze British Columbia’s dry soil moisture patterns throughout history, confirming that BC’s soil is drying out over time. With this information, we would know that droughts in BC are caused by climate change, and by using sustainable energies, we can decrease the rate of climate and dangerous occurrences, like wildfires.

Terminology

  • Soil Moisture Active Passive (SMAP) is an orbiting observatory launched by NASA designed to measure and monitor soil moisture across the Earth.
  • SMAP is measured in cubic meters of water per cubic meters of soil (m³/m³)
  • Root zone soil moisture refers to moisture found in the top 100 centimeters (1 meter) of soil.
  • 9 km EASE-Grid 2.0 refers to the grid system used to organize the data. EASE-Grid is a way to represent geographic data, and the “9 km” is the scale of each grid cell, which means that each grid cell covers an area of 9 kilometers on each side.

Analysis

Looking at Soil Moisture

On March 31st 2015, Prince George, British Columbia the root zone soil moisture using a grid system of 9 km EASE-Grid 2.0 measured 0.389 - 0.391 m³/m³ (figure 1.a). Whereas on September 18th, 2024 the same region of Prince George, British Columbia displayed a decline in root zone soil moisture to 0.223 - 0.226 m³/m³.
The decline in root zone soil moisture suggests that the top 100 cm or 1 m of soil is retaining much less water content when compared to 2015. This directly impacts vegetation as lower moisture causes them to get drier. Subsequently, the vegetation becomes more susceptible to catching fire, thereby becoming a greater fuel source for wildfires.
There are many factors that decrease soil moisture, but it is important to highlight that droughts where there are extended periods of below-average precipitation and overall climate change are significant leaders to reducing soil moisture. As plants and soil lose water faster than it can be replenished if precipitation is low and artificial climate change persists, it will undoubtedly pay negative effects on plant life and overall ecosystems.

Summary
